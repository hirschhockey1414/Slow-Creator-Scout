# Slow Creator Lens
### The Framework for Evaluating Creator-Founders
*Built on Slow Ventures' public thinking and investment memos. Every claim backed by real quotes.*

---

## Why This Exists

Creator investing is unprecedented. There are no comps, no established playbook, no standard framework for comparing a woodworking YouTuber to a dental orthodontist to a women's health founder. Slow Ventures is building the playbook in real time — and this is the lens they use to do it.

The creator economy is a $250B market. And unlike venture funding, it hasn't slowed down.

> "Despite the massive fall off in venture funding in the creator economy, creators are still growing like weeds. The value is actually being accrued to the creators." — Megan Lightcap

> "Creator investing is more similar to consumer underwriting than talent identification." — Megan Lightcap

> "The number one place AI is really giving leverage? To creators in our creator fund who can now build software on their own and are optimizing the sh*t out of their businesses with Claude." — Sam Lessin

---

## The Core Thesis

**Distribution precedes product.** Creators have a structural advantage over traditional founders: they build the audience before they build the business. Lower CAC, higher LTV, deeper trust.

**Trust has shifted from institutions to individuals.** This is the macro force behind everything. Creators are the beneficiaries.

> "They look at the media not as the end, but as the means to an end." — Megan Lightcap

> "The brand equity doesn't sit on the balance sheet of Feastables or Chamberlain Coffee — it sits with Emma, it sits with Jimmy." — Megan Lightcap

> "The story of entrepreneurship is being rewritten. The internet has empowered individuals with unprecedented reach and influence, turning them into trusted voices and community leaders of like-minded audiences." — Megan Lightcap

> "Individuals now have the tools, trust, and distribution to operate at a scale and speed that allows them to compete head on with institutions." — Megan Lightcap

This is why Slow invests in **holding companies**, not specific products. Whatever the creator focuses on next is what will succeed.

## The "Person and Theme" Test

Before any framework, Slow applies a two-part gut check:

1. **Person** — Is this a resilient, driven entrepreneur? Do they have founder-level discipline, not just creator energy?
2. **Theme** — Is the category important and growing? Are there compelling tailwinds or changing dynamics creating whitespace?

> "We talk a lot about 'person and theme' — and how we closely evaluate those criteria when deciding to invest in a founder or creator. We must have strong conviction in the person as a resilient, driven entrepreneur and the category in which they're operating as important and growing." — Megan Lightcap

When you add a creator's engaged community to this mix, the opportunity becomes even more compelling.

## The 5 Sourcing Categories

Thematic lenses for finding creators before they're obvious:

1. **Hobbies** — Skill-based respite with high propensity to spend. Duck hunting, chess, cycling, beekeeping. The audience doesn't just watch — they spend.

2. **Knowledge Gap** — Expert distilling complex or inaccessible information. Hormone health, physical therapy, parenting. Trust is built on credibility, not entertainment.

3. **Taboo Topics** — Safe space for conversations people can't have elsewhere. Sex/relationships, mental health, salary. Radical audience loyalty.

4. **Paradox of Choice** — Trusted curator shortcutting purchasing decisions. Skincare, healthy eating, romance novels. The audience follows because choosing is exhausting.

5. **Cultural Milieu** — Authentic representation of a community. NYC culture, Christianity, sorority rush. Identity-driven attachment.

## The 4 Due Diligence Pillars

Every creator investment evaluated against these four:

### 1. Creator Mindset
Thinks like a founder, not talent. Obsessed with building something bigger than themselves.

> "Red flag: 'I am just a YouTuber and that's my occupation.' We want people who self-identify as founder as much as creator." — Megan Lightcap

> "You're the entrepreneur, so you should be the one showing up." — Megan Lightcap

> "I've bet on myself too many times to give up the reins now… They answer the phone whenever I call, but they don't try to run the business for me." — Jonathan Katz Moses

### 2. Community Quality
Niche, vertical, deeply engaged. Stickiness denominated in skill or emotion, not entertainment.

> "Know your niche, know your audience — a lot of creators get caught up in knowing the demographics, but they don't know **why** people love them." — Megan Lightcap

> "The reason for attachment is often way more emotional." — Megan Lightcap

> "You're as popular as your least-viewed video in a year. And those people will support you in anything you do. So I don't need 50 million views. I need 100,000 loyal viewers and that's it." — Jonathan Katz Moses

### 3. Category Potential
Can you size the market? Real dollar volume moving through the space? Is there whitespace that the creator's specific angle uniquely unlocks?

> "There should be compelling tailwinds or changing dynamics that create whitespace for new value creation and capture." — Megan Lightcap

### 4. Traction and Scale
Proof of both audience growth AND business conversion. Has the creator actually sold anything?

> "Have you sold anything? Have you converted your audience? Test the bounds of your level of fandom." — Megan Lightcap

**The conversion proof standard:** Tayla Cannon generated 1,000+ PT inquiries and 350 beta applications from just two Instagram stories — before the product existed. That is what real conversion looks like.

## The Content-Commerce Flywheel

The structural advantage creators have over traditional founders:

**Content → Trust → Audience → Product Demand → Revenue → More Content**

This flywheel scales profitably without traditional ad spend. Jonathan Katz Moses grew KM Tools to $6M in revenue with **zero marketing spend** — his YouTube content IS his marketing.

> "Niche creators with deep audience trust can outcompete traditional brands in targeted verticals." — Slow Ventures

> "Personal narrative and authenticity remain powerful growth drivers for niche communities." — Slow Ventures

**Key principle:** Vertical integration = quality + trust. The creator who controls content, design, manufacturing, and fulfillment controls the brand.

---

## Key Principles

- **Audience alone is not enough** — need a real business underneath
- **Start with the margin math** — a big audience with bad unit economics is not a venture investment
- **Vertical integration = quality + trust** — the creator who controls their supply chain controls their brand
- **Brand is personal, but business should outlive you** — Gohar Khan (Next Admit) separated his personal brand from the business entity deliberately so the company survives him
- **Authenticity is not accidental, it's engineered** — the most authentic creators are the most intentional
- **IRL moat amidst AI** — physical products and experiences are defensible; content alone is not
- **Not all capital is equal — trust > terms** — creators should choose investors who understand them, not just the best valuation
- **"Broaden the aperture"** — the creator model extends beyond YouTube. Athletes, musicians, and other public figures with deep communities fit the same thesis. Michael Jordan extending IP beyond basketball is the athlete version of Martha Stewart.

> "Authenticity was the single most repeated word across all panels." — Creator CEO Summit recap

> "Kith is the best example of capitalism and art in harmony." — Megan Lightcap (on brand-building at the intersection of culture and commerce)

## The Martha Stewart Model

The north star for franchise potential:

- Multiple customer touchpoints by fandom level (casual magazine reader → cooking class attendee → product buyer)
- Expansion across product categories
- "Knew what her audience wanted before they knew it themselves"
- Built an entire ecosystem serving audiences at every engagement level

The modern version: **Steven Bartlett's three-pillar model** — media franchise + incubation arm + investment fund. Each pillar feeds the others. Audience insights inform what businesses to build. Businesses create content. Content grows the audience.

The old-school version: **Margaritaville** — Jimmy Buffett's cultural authority built into restaurants, hotels, resorts, retirement communities, and products. A multi-billion dollar ecosystem built before "creator" was a word.

> "These pillars create a powerful flywheel where audience insights from the media ecosystem inform business incubation and investment decisions — compounding both impact and system-wide enterprise value over time." — Megan Lightcap

---

## Red Flags

- Creator identifies primarily as a content creator, not a founder
- Audience attached to entertainment, not skill or identity
- Product is merch, brand deals, or courses — not something the creator built and owns
- No proof of audience conversion (no sales, no signups, no waitlist)
- Sends a manager to investor meetings instead of showing up themselves
- Category has no real dollar volume moving through it
- Business depends entirely on the creator's continued presence with no franchise potential
- Growth driven by virality, not community depth
- "I don't have enough time" — this is the founder filter. Real founders make time.

### Tayla Cannon — The Conversion Proof

**Platform:** ~140K Instagram (grew from 10K in February 2024 — 14x in 18 months)
**Business:** Rebuildr — three integrated pillars:

**Investment:** $1.1M

**The Tayla Signal:** 1,000+ PTs inquired and 350 applied to beta test Rebuildr after only two Instagram stories. That is conversion before the product was built.

## The Creator Score

**The Creator Score measures not where a creator is today, but how far they can go.**

Six dimensions, each scored 1–10. Maximum 60 points. Use the leaderboard to compare creators across completely different verticals on the same plane.

---

### Dimension 1: Disruption Angle (1–10)
*Is this creator attacking their industry in a genuinely novel way — or just doing it better?*

The best investments don't improve the existing model. They make the existing model look broken. Tayla Cannon didn't build better PT — she reframed PT as something proactive and daily, the way mental health evolved from crisis intervention to ongoing wellness. The Bentist didn't make better toothpaste — he declared that 150 years of oral care is built on lies and built the science-backed alternative.

| Score | Signal |
|---|---|
| 1–3 | Doing what incumbents do, slightly better |
| 4–6 | Clear point of differentiation, but within existing category norms |
| 7–9 | Reframing the category entirely |
| 10 | Category destruction — makes the old model look broken |

---

### Dimension 2: Community Depth (1–10)
*Is the audience attached to the world the creator built, or just the creator themselves?*

Deep community survives platform changes, content pivots, and product launches. Shallow community disappears the moment the algorithm changes. The question is: why do people follow this creator? If the answer is entertainment, that's a 3. If the answer is identity, skill, or transformation, that's a 9.

| Score | Signal |
|---|---|
| 1–3 | Follows for entertainment, low repeat engagement |
| 4–6 | Genuine interest in the niche, moderate loyalty |
| 7–9 | Skill-based or identity-based attachment, high engagement |
| 10 | Community outlives the creator — it's a movement, not an audience |

---

### Dimension 3: Conversion Proof (1–10)
*Has the creator actually sold something to their audience?*

An unmonetized audience is a hypothesis. A monetized audience is a business. This is the most important near-term signal.

| Score | Signal |
|---|---|
| 1–3 | No product, no sales, no waitlist |
| 4–6 | Early product with modest traction |
| 7–9 | Real revenue, repeat customers, demand outpacing supply |
| 10 | Sold out repeatedly / waitlist / audience demands product before it exists |

---

### Dimension 4: Operator Signals (1–10)
*Does this creator think and talk like a founder?*

Listen for: systems thinking, market framing, language about scaling, willingness to hire, understanding of unit economics. Watch for: vertical integration, multiple business lines, turning down deals that don't fit the long-term vision.

> "Creator economies are maturing into sustainable, defensible businesses — blurring the lines between influencer and founder." — Slow Ventures

| Score | Signal |
|---|---|
| 1–3 | "I just make content." No business language. |
| 4–6 | Aware of business opportunity, hasn't fully committed to founder identity |
| 7–9 | Talks about team, operations, market, unit economics |
| 10 | Vertically integrated operation, multiple business lines, institutionalized innovation |

---

### Dimension 5: Category Expansion Logic (1–10)
*Does the brand naturally grow beyond the first product?*

The first product is proof of concept. The question is whether the creator's authority extends across a broader category — or whether it hits a one-product ceiling. Tayla's three-pillar model (Certification → Platform → Marketplace) is the template: each pillar serves the same community at a different depth of engagement.

| Score | Signal |
|---|---|
| 1–3 | One product, one use case, no obvious adjacent moves |
| 4–6 | Clear path to 2–3 products in the same category |
| 7–9 | Brand authority extends across multiple verticals |
| 10 | Full Martha Stewart / Steven Bartlett potential — multiple categories, multiple touchpoints, ecosystem thinking |

---

### Dimension 6: Franchise Ceiling (1–10)
*What's the realistic $ ceiling on this creator's holding company if everything goes right?*

This is the synthesis dimension. It asks: what does this become?

| Score | Signal |
|---|---|
| 1–3 | $1–10M lifestyle business |
| 4–6 | $10–50M niche brand |
| 7–9 | $50–200M category-defining company |
| 10 | $200M+ — institutional, multi-category, generational brand |
